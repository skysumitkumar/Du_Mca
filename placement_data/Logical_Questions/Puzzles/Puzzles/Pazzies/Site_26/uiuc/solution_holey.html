Solution

Question:
This puzzle is really cool. If you drill a cylindrical hole, that is six inches long, through the center of a sphere, what is the remaining volume of the sphere? The diameter of the cylinder is not negligible. Explain your answer. Yes, the data is sufficient!

Answer:
36*pi cubic inches

After writing down some funky math equations for the volume of a sphere with radius R, and a cylinder of height 6, and the volume of the 2 dome caps above and below the cylinder, all terms cancel out to leave 36*pi.

Therefore, no matter what the radius of the sphere is, if you cut out a cylinder through the center such that the height is 6 inches, the remaining volume will always be 36*pi!

Let R be the radius of the sphere. The radius of the cylindrical hole will then be sqrt(R^2 - 9). The height of the spherical caps at each end of the cylinder will be R-3. To determine what is left after the cylinder and the caps have been removed, we do the following:

Add the volume of the cylinder 6*PI*(R^2 - 9)
Add twice the volume of the spherical caps where the volume of a spherical cap is PI*A*(3r^2 + A^2)/6, where A is the altitude of the cap (R -3), and r is the radius of the cap, sqrt(R^2 - 9)
Subtract this from the volume of the sphere (4/3)*PI*R^3

You are left with 36*PI, the volume of the remaining solid. As you can see, the volume of this remaining solid is constant, regardless of the cylindrical hole's radius, or the radius of the sphere!
