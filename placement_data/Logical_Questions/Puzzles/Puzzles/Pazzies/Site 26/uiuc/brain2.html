  2. Four Spiders (Master)

    Four cannibalistic spiders are placed one in each corner of a square room. The length of each side is 10 feet. They are released simultaneously and each spider starts moving directly towards the neighbour on its right at the same constant speed. How far will each spider move before they all meet in the middle of the room? Explain your answer.

    Submitted by: Anthony StJohn. Thanks!

  3. Two Camels (Intermediate)

    An Arab sheikh tells his two sons to race their camels to a distant city to see who will inherit his fortune. The one whose camel is slower will win. The brothers, after wandering aimlessly for days, ask a wise man for advice. After hearing his advice they jump onto the camels and race as fast as they can to the distant city. What did the wise man say to them?

    Solved by: hulljoe, G K, ninee & MaxHal44

  4. Hand Shake (Expert)

    A couple invites 4 couples for dinner. When all the guests have arrived, everybody shakes hands with the person he/she does not know. The host then asks everybody (except himself, of course) how many people they shook hands with. Each one of them replies with a different number. How many people did the hostess shake hands with? Why?

    Comments: You cannot make any assumptions about how many people the hostess knows. There is only one logical soluti on if you consider all the constraints of this puzzle!

    Submitted by: Harshit Patel. Thanks!
    Solved by: Claude Gepp, Mike Dickheiser & Joe Hullender

  5. Cheesy Puzzle (Master)

    A cube of cheese is divided into 27 sub-cubes. A mouse starts at one corner and eats each sub-cube, one at a time. Can it finish in the center of the cube? Note that the mouse can only go from a sub-cube to another one that has a common face with it.

    Solved by: Kalyankrishnan & John Tripp

This would drastically reduce the time for the commute. To the Mayor's surprise, after the expressway was opened, none of these people traveled from point A to B any more.

    What was the reason?

  7. The Coin Game (Master)

    Alice and Bob are playing a coin game. The rules are as follows: There is a circular table with radius R. There is an infinite supply of identical coins, each with radius r (where r < R). Alice and Bob take turns placing coins on the table. The coins cannot overlap, and they must entirely lie on the table. The first person who cannot place a coin on the table loses.

    Does Alice or Bob have a winning strategy? If so, what is it? Your solution should work for all possible values of r and R.

    Comments: A winning strategy means that a certain player is guaranteed to win every time, as long as he/she follows this strategy.

    Solved by: John Tripp, Abhijit Rashingkar & Patrick Govern

  9. Trekker (Expert)

    A trekker begins her ascent at 6am until she reaches the summit later that day. She walks at varying speeds, with frequent stops along the way. After spending a few days atop the mountain, she begins her descent at 6am one fine day. She follows her original path, and walks down at varying speeds with frequent stops. Her walking patterns on the way up, and on the way down have no connection whatsoever, except that she probably walks faster on the way down than on the way up.

    Prove that there is a spot along the path that she will occupy on both her treks at exactly the same time of the day.

    Solved by: John Tripp, RMontgomery & Kenny Compton

  10. The Mad Hatter (Intermediate)

    The March Hare and the Mad Hatter were sipping their eggnog and watching the crowd when Alice happened to glance in the Hare's direction and ask, "Why are you giving me such an angry look?"
    "I'm not *giving* it to you, I'm giving it *back*," replied the Hare.
    "I didn't look crossly at you."
    "Well, *somebody* did," the Hare said, turning to glare at the Hatter.
    Just then, someone came up from behind and put his hands over the Hatter's eyes.
    "Guess who!" said the newcomer in a thin, flat voice.
    The Hatter froze for a moment and declared, rather coldly, "I have no use for practical jokers."
    "Ha! Neither have I," retorted the stranger, still keeping his hands over the Hatter's eyes.
    At that, the Hatter seemed to accept the challenge of the game and started asking a series of questions in a manner that mingled hope with care.

    Question: "Ahem. Would you, by chance, be in a black suit this evening?"
    Answer: "I would, but not by chance, by design."

    Q: "I presume you're a member of all the posh clubs?"
    A: "Afraid not. Never even been invited."

    Q: "Surely you're better than average?"
    A: "Yes, indeed!"

    Q: "Not spotted, I hope?"
    A: "Knock wood."

    Q: "Married?"
    A: "No, happy."

    Who is behind the Mad Hatter?
    Solved by: Ted Scearcy, Conrad and Marian & Oren Fix

  12. Stand Still! (Expert)

  13. Ali Baba (Expert)

    This is one of Raymond Smullyan's cooler logic puzzles.

    Twenty of Ali Baba's thieves once captured an enormous plunder consisting of diamonds, emeralds, rubies, gold coins, chocolate and other goodies. Then came the question of dividing the loot so that each of the twenty believed he had his fair share. Now, some prefer jewels, some prefer chocolate and so on. In other words, different members will have different ideas as to what constitutes one twentieth of the loot. So it won't work for one person to simply divide the loot into what he considers twenty equal parts and pass the parts around; many would be dissatisfied. However, it is possible to make a division such that each person feels satisfied that he has got at least one twentieth of the loot. Of course there is nothing special about the number twenty; the same could be done with a hundred people, or any number of people. For example, the way to do it with only two people is as follows : One person divides the material into what he considers two equal parts and lets the other person choose which part he wants. Then both are bound to be satisfied. The question is how do you adapt this to twenty people?

    Solved by: John Tripp
