Feature

Heat spreaders and fans diffuse hot spots

Consumer electronics need to run—and look—cool. Heat-dissipation techniques affect systems' look, feel, and noise. Most designs use either a combination of fans and heat exchangers or a purely passive heat spreader.

By Margery Conner, Technical Editor -- , 3/17/2005

AT A GLANCE
  • Fans are useful as active dissipators, but quiet operation and low power consumption are necessary.
  • Designers can efficiently use fans with passive heat exchangers.
  • Graphite heat spreaders can replace fans in systems that combine quiet operation and slim profiles.

Handheld consumer devices can give off only a fairly nominal amount of heat before becoming "too hot to handle," or they simply risk making a user uncomfortable. In addition, consumers are starting to expect their electronic devices to operate virtually silently, which can rule out incorporating a fan, a constraint at odds with the devices' higher processor speeds and denser packaging. Fortunately, designers can reduce or eliminate both hot spots and audible system noise using new heat-removal methods and technologies that combine to remove excessive heat and require a minimum of space, moving parts, and power.

For systems with less stringent size limitations, a combination of active and passive heat-removal components is common. Notebook computers usually use one or more fans, a heat pipe, and a heat exchanger. A heat pipe comprises a metal tube encasing both a liquid and a wick. The wick pulls the heat from the processor into the liquid, beginning a cycle of vaporization and condensation that moves the heat through the pipe until it reaches the heat sink, in which airflow from the fan dissipates the heat.

Figure 1 shows a notebook processor on an attachment block, which transfers heat through a heat pipe to a heat exchanger, which a fan in turn cools. The advantage of using a heat pipe over simply blowing ambient air over the hot spot is that a fan can cool the processor without being near it. However, a fan introduces two undesirable elements: moving parts with attendant unreliability and audible noise. In the past, fan control for laptops was simple: A thermistor measured the temperature at a system hot spot, such as the processor, and once the temperature exceeded a threshold beyond which performance or reliability was compromised, the system fan turned on and stayed on, running at a constant speed.

By including a thermal-management IC in the system design, fans no longer have to run full at full speed continuously, once the system reaches the trigger-temperature. According to Paul Errico, product manager for Analog Devices' dBcool line, "The chip measures the temperature of the CPU or other hot spot in the notebook and will turn the fan speed at a point that cools the hot spot down but keeps the fan speed at its lowest possible speed to minimize mean time between failure and lower power consumption.'' Communication is through the two-wire SMBus (System Management Bus). You can obtain thermal-management ICs from such companies as Analog Devices, National Semiconductor, and Maxim.

Introducing a mechanical fan into an otherwise-all-electronic system immediately makes the fan the most likely failure mechanism. Yet, for many systems, multiple fans are the most efficient, cost-effective heat-removal device. For these systems, using a fan with built-in intelligence, such as the Intellifan, allows the system to compensate for variable fan life, predict component failure, and schedule preventive maintenance. Whereas thermal-management ICs can monitor hot spots across the board and then control the speed of multiple fans, an Intellifan's intelligence resides in the individual fans' enabling self-monitoring (Figure 2).

"What causes failure in a fan is not the electronics; it's the mechanical bearing system," says Chak Chakir, product manager for Intellifan. "There is intelligence inside an Intellifan that can communicate with a [system] software-diagnostic routine." He says that, if the fan's performance degrades over time, you can use the SMBus to run the fan at a slightly higher speed to compensate for that degradation. Meanwhile, you can make plans to replace the fan. Similarly, a system with multiple fans can compensate for one fan's degradation by running another system fan at a higher speed.

Although reliability concerns have always been a factor in designing with fans, fan noise is becoming equally important in consumer electronics. Developers of designs targeting applications that previously accepted some fan noise are now developing designs without fans. Again, the ubiquitous notebook computer serves as a good example. Currently, notebooks have one to three fans, but Analog Devices' Errico sees this trend changing. "Many manufacturers are looking for ways to differentiate their designs. Low system noise is becoming a market-differentiating technology,'' he says.

Richard Terril, senior manager of Spartan Solutions at Xilinx, agrees. The Spartan product line targets consumer-home-theater products, and Terril uses as an example the user experience in a home theater with a large flat-panel plasma display: "If you're watching Braveheart, a little fan noise won't be noticeable. But if it's a quiet scene in Terms of Endearment, you don't want fan noise in the background.'' In addition to reliability issues and noise, a fanless system saves space, an overriding concern in many consumer devices, such as high-end laptops, audio players, and cell phones.

The first step in removing hot spots is to minimize their creation. Terril points out, "Power is a budgeted item, so it's not that you have to be low, but that you have to be low enough. In a system, everything's consuming power. Look at a notebook: You know you'll have the display, the disk drive, the processor, the memory—all consuming power. Your first step in accounting for system power is to know your power budget.''

First, do no harm

The terms "handheld" and "laptop" imply that these devices make contact with human skin, which imposes some constraints on how much heat they can dissipate externally, in turn affecting the system's power budget. The density of heat-generating devices within the unit compounds the problem: At the upper limit of human comfort, 50°C is as high as the surface of a system should go. Skin damage can occur at 55°C. If the ambient temperature is 25°C, then a maximum of 25°C is available to radiantly transfer heat from outside the system to the external air. Intel uses a straw-man example of a small handheld device of about 300 cm3 under average conditions to be able to dissipate 5W (Reference 1).

Even with constant vigilance regarding power consumption, a system with high-performance processing, a display, and memory storage will likely exceed the natural power-dissipation capabilities of a handheld system. Fortunately, recent advances in graphite-heat-spreader technology are increasing the purely passive cooling capability of systems.

A heat sink, one form of a heat exchanger, is a finned structure that designers usually use with a fan blowing air through the fins. The fins increase the surface area of the sink, which attaches to the heat-producing component, often a processor. On the other hand, a heat spreader takes heat from a hot spot and spreads it around. It can take a 50°C or so hot spot and uniformly spread the heat so that the hot-spot temperature drops by 5°, according to Julian Norley, PhD, chief scientist at Graftech. To put this reduction in temperature into perspective, it is sufficient to reach a temperature that a user can comfortably hold (Figure 3).

Graphite is well-suited as a heat-spreader material because it's anisotropic, meaning that it conducts heat well along its x and y axes but poorly in the z axis. For example, the in-plane, 2-D thermal conductivity of graphite is as high as 500W/m-K—about the same as copper—whereas the z-axis conductivity is less than 10W/m-K. This anisotropic characteristic allows the spreader to conduct heat longitudinally away from the source. Plus, it's lightweight: For the same thermal conductivity as copper, graphite weighs about one-fifth as much as copper. Conventional heat-spreader materials, such as aluminum and copper, move heat in all directions, but, due to high contact resistance, they inefficiently transfer heat from components. Keep in mind, however, that graphite can be more brittle than aluminum or copper.

Spreaders cool passively

According to Graftech's Norley, the power level that creates a hot spot requiring a spreader can vary. One ultrathin laptop that uses Graftech's SpreaderShield heat spreader in a purely passive cooling system dissipates a 12W hot spot that the processor causes. However, another application might require a spreader at a lower temperature. Such an application, for example, would be a cell phone. For an ear, a relatively tender body part, a concentrated 5W hot spot would be uncomfortable. In addition, recent studies have examined whether laptop heat affects male fertility when men balance the devices on their laps instead of on tabletops or desktops. The results of these studies may presage the need to further reduce consumer-system heat in those systems that come into body contact (Reference 2).

Preventing hot spots in flat-panel displays is another application for spreader shields. A plasma display has no fan or a very quiet one to enhance the user viewing experience, rather than targeting the avoidance of discomfort or the use of a small form factor. Norley points out that plasma displays are especially susceptible to hot-spot burn-in. "For example, if the panel is displaying a picture with a logo in the corner for an extended period, you've got a hot spot. White is the hottest because all of the pixels are on, and you'll get burn-in at that localized area of maximum heat.'' Because no one can predict when a hot spot will occur, plasma displays require a spreader over the back of the display. For example, one large plasma display's 102-in. back requires a spreader.

Spreaders reach their maximum heat-dissipating capability at about 20W. As a rule, devices with hot spots greater than 20W need an active system with fans. But this figure has been steadily increasing, and Norley expects this trend to continue. "The first laptop to rely on the eSpreader diffused about 5W" he says, adding that the most recent laptop diffused 15W.
