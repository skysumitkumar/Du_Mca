High-performance DSOs untangle serial-data streams

As electronic systems depend more and more on serial communication, sophisticated digital scopes, powered by clever software, are becoming essential equipment.

By Dan Strassberg, Contributing Technical Editor, EDN North America -- , 6/23/2005

New electronic technology often begets even newer electronic technology. Few more impressive examples of this maxim exist than the jitter-measurement- and serial-data-stream-analysis capabilities of modern high-end DSOs (digital storage oscilloscopes). These sophisticated instruments grew out of a confluence of enabling technologies, many of which originated with the PC (see sidebar, "Scope technologies"), and a significant engineering need.

As system builders realized that parallel buses had hit a wall, their subsequent move to serial interfaces created demand for scopes that could analyze serial-data streams. Although equipment that incorporates parallel buses will continue in production for years, the use of such buses in new designs is declining rapidly. Industry observers insist that the last major holdout—memory ICs—will soon hop aboard the serial bus.

You might think that cramming more data per unit of time down fewer wires or optical fibers would complicate the task of transmitting information without introducing errors. But, taking all of the problems into account, sending data in serial streams is a winning approach. A key reason: Even though serial buses feature shorter clock periods or UIs (unit intervals) than parallel buses, serial-bus data travels in packets whose duration is many UIs. Therefore, despite much higher clock frequencies, timing is less critical.

In addition, serial buses use embedded-clocking techniques, the most popular of which is 8B/10B (8-bit/10-bit) coding. These techniques cleverly mix clock and data signals, doing away with separate clock lines and relaxing requirements on skew—timing differences among signals that ideally reach their destinations simultaneously.

Sophisticated but simple

The software that runs in DSOs to provide information on serial-data-link performance is extremely sophisticated, yet for those usaccustomed to working with powerful mathematical software, using it is not especially complex. Writing programs is generally unnecessary, although several packages allow users to create custom routines by recording mouse clicks, by using a graphical metaphor, or by writing text-based scripts.

Each of the three major manufacturers of high-performance DSOs offers its own serial-data-analysis packages. In addition, each of the companies works with a specialized supplier, ASA (Amherst Systems Associates), to provide jitter-analysis software. ASA's software runs on a large number of Agilent, LeCroy, and Tektronix scopes and is available both through the scope manufacturers and directly from ASA.

For years, ASA was vocal about its affection for Hewlett-Packard's 54720A sequential-equivalent-time-sampling scope. That scope is no longer in production, but ASA's Web site (www.amherst-systems.com) lists more than a dozen DSOs that ASA has approved for use with its software. Unlike the 54720A, nearly all of these scopes, which come from all three major high-end DSO manufacturers, sample in real time, because ASA's latest products—including the flagship M1 V4 package—now emphasize analysis techniques based on data acquired in real-time.

Nevertheless, equivalent-time-sampling scopes (often simply called sampling scopes) remain very much in the jitter-analysis picture. For example, Agilent's 86100C digital- communications analyzer (a descendent of the HP 54720A) is available in a version called the DCA-J, which expressly targets jitter analysis (see picture). For similar applications, Tektronix offers its CSA 8000B communications-signal analyzer, whereas LeCroy has just introduced the revolutionary SDA 100G. By intentionally undersampling their input signals, these scopes offer bandwidths as high as 100 GHz, nearly seven times that of the widest-bandwidth real-time scope. Rise times as short as approximately 5 psec accompany the extraordinary bandwidth.

Verifying standards compliance

Compliance testing has also become a commonplace feature in DSOs' serial-data-analysis software. The packages check compliance in accordance with standards established by the consortia and SIGs (special-interest groups) that control the various high-speed serial-bus protocols and implementation details.

Typically, such tests are quite involved. Without the use of prepackaged routines, design-validation engineers could spend weeks developing protocols and more time testing preproduction units for compliance, only to find—perhaps much later—that their protocols contained subtle flaws that would invalidate the test results. Because the responsible standards groups have vetted the test methods embodied in the DSO manufacturers' packages, validation engineers can have much greater confidence in their test results.

Real-time scopes have become popular in serial-bus debugging because they possess several attributes that can save system developers from weeks of frustration. One is the ability to reveal how system behavior changes from one UI to the next. Also, when equipped with deep memory, a real-time scope can capture and repeatedly replay long records of activity, during which the system under test may have misbehaved only once or twice for periods as brief as, say, 1 nsec. These records often contain clues to the causes of these elusive phenomena.

Moreover, by capturing and replaying stimulus waveforms that provoke the aberrant behaviors, an engineer can often make a problem appear frequently enough for its true cause—and remedy—to become obvious. A scope that acquires 20G samples/sec and has a waveform memory that holds 100M samples can acquire 5-msec-duration records at its highest sampling rate. If you slow the sampling rate to 5G samples/sec—usually adequate to capture 1-nsec-duration phenomena—the scope can capture records whose duration is 20 msec.

Scope technologies

A partial list of the technologies that enable scopes to analyze serial-data artifacts—principally, jitter—starts with the concept of building DSOs (digital storage oscilloscopes) around PCs. This approach allows the instruments to gather data and run analysis software that runs on PCs.

The idea of building a PC into a DSO became economically practical only after the mass PC market made the inner workings of PCs truly inexpensive. Low-cost, high-speed-memory ICs, an offshoot of PC technology, also helped to make possible today’s high-performance scopes, although DSO manufacturers had to design some of the instruments’ fastest memory chips on their own.

Scope makers also turned to custom design to create some analog components, including high-speed ADCs and vertical amplifiers. For some DSO manufacturers, new IC-fabrication technologies, such as SiGe (silicon germanium), made these system components practical.
