    23. Abhishek, your method assumes that the heavy ball is MUCH heavier than any of the other balls. It is certainly not a general solution. In general 4 balls are going to be heavier than 3 balls, so already your first step is flawed. Example: group1 = 10 + 10 + 15 = 35, group2 = 10 + 10 + 10 + 10 = 40. The heavy group is group2, but the heavy ball is in group1.

      To make your method work in general for all possible weight distributions the weight of the heavy ball needs to be more than 4 times the weight of the second heaviest ball (which covers the worst case senario for step 1: group1 = heavy + ~0 + ~0, group2 = Y + Y + Y + Y, where “heavy” is such that group1 > group2 -> heavy > 4 * Y).

    27. Q1. This is a trick/trap question. It encourages the candidate to make two (maybe fatal) assumptions: First that the scale is a traditional (balancing) one and second that 6 of the balls are of equal weight. The tip-off that this is a trick question is the mention of three (3) possible weighing attempts. It has been shown above that if the two assumptions are correct, the task can be done in two (2) attempts. Why inflate the possible attempts if testing a candidate’s logic? In an oral examination, this is a test in requirements gathering. Ask to clarify the assumptions. In a written exam, this is a test of managing expectations / documentation. State the assumptions, then solve the problem.

    42. I can’t believe the different answers for the 7 ball exercise! There are several of you who skipped steps completely. Assume that if it is a question by a legitimate company that it will not be a trick question. If you doubt it, ask. The solution is simple:

      1. Put 3 balls on one side and 3 balls on the other. If it balances you have the heavy one in your hand. If not goto step 2.

      2. Remove 2 balls, one from each side and discard the one from the lightest side. If the scale balances you have the heavy ball in your hand. If not goto step 3.

      3. Remove 2 balls, one from each side and discard the one from the lightest side. If the scale balances you have the heavy ball in your hand. If not the heavy ball is on the scale on the heaviest side.

      It take 3 steps Max. There is no way to do it with 2 steps max or 1 step max, but it can be done in 1 step min or 2 steps min.

      Rich

    56. I have a scale and 7 balls. 1 ball is heavier than all the rest. How do I determine the heaviest ball with only 3 possible weighing attempts?

      Answer: There are 2 possible solutions:–

      1) Weigh the first 3 balls. Then weigh the second 3 balls. If their values are the same, then we can assume the 7th ball is the heaviest. Weight that and that is done in three steps.

      2) Weigh the first 3 balls. Then weigh the second 3 balls. If their values are different, subtract the largest from the smallest. Weigh the 7th ball. Take that value subtracted and add it to the 7th ball. This is also done in 3 steps.

      Global data is shared between all the threads of a program and could lead to a ‘race condition’ if not properly locked/synchronised. One way of handling global data in a multi-threaded program is to use ‘critical sections’ or ‘mutexes’ for data synchronisation.

    64. I have a scale and 7 balls. 1 ball is heavier than all the rest. How do I determine: the heaviest ball with only 3 possible weighing attempts:

      Weigh six balls using a balance beam type scale. If the weight is equal, the ball not weighed is the heaviest, otherwise move to step two. We know that a ball on the right or left of the beam is heavier. Let’s assume the right is heavier. Remove one ball from the right and reweigh. If the weight is equal the removed ball is the heaviest. Otherwise the scale will tell us which is heavier. We have now solved the problem two steps versus three.
