Question:
Construct a regular pentagon by using only a compass and a straight edge. Send us a description of your construction along with a proof (or an adequate explanation) showing that your construction is correct.

Answer:
Thanks to Glenn for these three points:

Note 1: a straight edge does *not* contain a scale. Measuring distances by using a scale is not an allowed operation in classical compass/straightedge constructions.

Note 2: There is no solution to the regular heptagon nor the regular nonagon. A regular n-gon is constructible using only a straightedge and a compass if and only if n is 3, 5, 17, 257, or 65537 (due to Gauss I believe). These values can be obtained from the formula 2^2^k + 1 where k is an integer from 0 through 4. These values are known as the Fermat primes (k=5 does not yield a prime).

Note 3: They are called Fermat primes after Pierre Fermat (the "t" is silent) who in the 17th century conjectured that all numbers of the form 2^2^k are prime. The conjecture was shown to be false when the value for k=5 was factored in the first half of the 20'th century.

Here is Lyle Ishol's solution:

PENTAGON CONSTRUCTION

The problem is to construct a regular pentagon (all five sides and angles equal) using only a pencil, compass and straight edge. I would expect this is very difficult unless you have seen it before somewhere, so even if you forgot how to do it, you could try a few things and rediscover it.

SOLUTION

Convention: A line connecting point "A" with point "B" is referred to as "line AB", and its length is "AB". Also the distance between point "A" and point "B" is "AB" whether or not there is a line. An arc is referred to as "arc AB", meaning the shorter of the two possible arcs connecting "A" and "B", where "A" and "B" are point on a circle or part of a circle.

1. Draw a circle somewhere on your paper of the size that will circumscribe the pentagon. Label the center "O".

2. Draw a diameter approximately vertical, obviously passing through "O".

3. Using the compass and straight edge, draw a diameter perpendicular to the first diameter.

4. Label the points where the diameters intersect the circle "A", "B", "C", and "D", starting with "A" at the top and going clockwise.

5. Using the compass and straight edge, bisect line "OB", and label the point "E".

6. With the compass, use "E" as the center and distance "AE" as the radius, draw an arc intersecting line "DO". Label the point "F".

7. With the compass, use "A" as the center and distance "AF" as the radius, draw an arc intersecting arc "AB" and arc "AD". Label the points "G" and "H".

8. Using the straightedge, draw a line connect points "A" and "G" and points "A" and "H". These lines are two sides of the pentagon.

9. With the compass, use "G" as the center and "AG" as the radius (i.e., don’t change the adjustment on the compass), draw an arc intersecting arc "CD", and label the point "I".

10. Move the center to "H", and use "AH" as the radius to draw an arc intersecting arc "BC". Label the point "J".

11. Connect points "G" and "I", "I" and "J", and "J" and "H". This completes the polygon.

Considering that you have to use finite-sized lines, arcs, and points, errors could accumulate so that if you use "I" as a center and swing an arc through arc "BC", it may miss point "J" by a few line widths. However, it can be proven that the construction is exact.

PROOF

There may be more elegant solutions, but a brute force solution is presented below.

1. Use the figure and point labels as drawn to construct the polygon, and assume the radius, "AO", equals one is some system of units.

2. "OE" = ½ and"AO" = 1, are the sides of a right triangle.

3. The hypotenuse, "AE" = (12 + (½)2)½ = (5/4)½ = 5 ½/2.

4. As "EF" = "AE", "OF" = "EF" - "OE" = "AE" -"OE" = 5 ½/2 -1/2 = ½(5 ½-1).

5. "AO" and "OF" are sides of a right triangle with hypotenuse "AF". Thus, "AF" can be calculated as follows: "AF" = (12 + (½(5 ½-1))2)½ = (1 + 1/4(5 - 2x5 ½ + 1))½ = (1 + ½(3 - 5 ½))½

= (5/2 - 5 ½/2)½.

6. AS "AF" = "AG", "AG" = (5/2 - 5 ½/2)½, which we allege is the length of each of the sides of the pentagon.

7. We draw line "OG", and note that "AG" plus "AO" and "OG" form a triangle with sides 1, 1, and (5/2 - 5 ½/2)½. We name the angle "AOG" formed at the center of the circle by lines "AO" and "OG".

8. Bisect line "AG" and label the point "K". Since sides "AO", "AK", and "KO" form a triangle whose sides equal the sides of another triangle, "GO", "GK", and "KO" , The two angles at point "K" are equal and sum to 180o, they are 90o angles. "AK" = ½ of "AG" or ½ (5/2 - 5 ½/2)½, and from AK2 + KO2 = 1, KO2 = 1 - 1/4(5/2 - 5 ½/2) = (3 + 5 ½)/8, and KO = ((3 + 5 ½)/8)½.

Sin AOK = ½ (5/2 - 5 ½/2)½ = ((5 - 5 ½)/8)½ and cos AOK = ((3 + 5 ½)/8)½.

Sin AOG = sin 2AOK = 2sin AOK cos AOK = 2 ((5 - 5 ½)/8)½((3 + 5 ½)/8)½ = ((5 + 5 ½)/8)½.

If it a regular pentagon, AOG = 72o, which can be checked easily enough with a calculator, but a calculator isn’t exact due to the limitation on the number of significant figures. So we continue by looking up the formula for sin 5AOG:

Sin 5AOG = 5 sin AOG - 20 sin3AOG +16 sin5AOG.

For convenience, we write down sin2AOG = (5 + 5 ½)/8, then

 

 

sin3AOG = (5 + 5 ½)/8((5 + 5 ½)/8)½, and sin5AOG = ((5 + 5 ½)/8)2((5 + 5 ½)/8)½, and

sin 5AOG = 5((5 + 5 ½)/8)½ - 20(5 + 5 ½)/8((5 + 5 ½)/8)½ +16 ((5 + 5 ½)/8)2((5 + 5 ½)/8)½.

Factor out ((5 + 5 ½)/8)½ to get

sin 5AOG =((5 + 5 ½)/8)½ (5- 20(5 + 5 ½)/8 +16 ((5 + 5 ½)/8)2).

Evaluate the expression (5- 20(5 + 5 ½)/8 +16 ((5 + 5 ½)/8)2) to get "0", so

sin 5AOG =((5 + 5 ½)/8)½(0) = 0, and therefore 5AOG = 0o or 360o and knowing from the construction that AOG is something less than 90o, 5AOG can only be 360o, so the solution is exact.
