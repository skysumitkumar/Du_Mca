  2. Dreamy Women (Expert)

    In a fantasy world, women were bestowed with an exceptional power: every night, they could dream about all the men, other than their own husband, in the country who were thieves. For example, if some woman dreamt about 5 men, she could not be sure about how many men were really thieves: it could be 5 or 6 (because she did not know anything about her husband). However, they could not discuss their dreams with each other because that was considered a taboo subject. One day, the queen of the country declared: "I know a number of men in the country are thieves. I want their wives to kill them."

    Now the situation was as such: the women could not talk to each other about their dreams. However, if some woman killed her husband, it was proclaimed throughout the country.

    Nothing happened after the declaration of the queen before the sixteenth day, when a number of women killed their husbands on the same morning.

    How many husbands were killed? You must include an explanation of your answer.

    Submitted by: Suman Nath. Thanks!
    Solved by: Richard Meffley, Hjallti & Vishal Maru

  4. Two Clocks (Intermediate)

    This is a Raymond Smullyan puzzle.

    Two friends, whom we will call Arthur and Robert, were curators at the Museum of American History. Both were born in the month of May, one in 1932 and the other a year later.

    Each was in charge of a beautiful antique clock. Both of the clocks worked pretty well, considering their ages, but one of them lost ten seconds an hour and the other gained ten seconds an hour.

    On one bright day in January, the two friends set both clocks right at exactly 12 noon.

    ``You realize,'' said Arthur, ``that the clocks will start drifting apart, and they won't be together again until---let's see---why, on the very day you will be 47 years old. Am I right?''

    Robert then made a short calculation. ``That's right!'' he said.

    Who is older, Arthur or Robert?

    Solved by: Oren Fix, Fred Franzw a & Jennifer Yu

  6. Caliban's Will (Master)

    This is an original creation by M.H. Newman

    When Caliban's will was opened it was found to contain the following clause:

    "I leave ten of my books to each of Low, Y.Y., and 'Critic,' who are to choose in a certain order.

    No person who has seen me in a green tie is to choose before Low.

    If Y.Y. was not in Oxford in 1920 the first chooser never lent me an umbrella.

    If Y.Y. or 'Critic' has second choice, 'Critic' comes before the one who first fell in love."

    Unfortunately Low, Y.Y., and 'Critic' could not remember any of the relevant facts; but the family solicitor pointed out that, assuming the problem to be properly constructed (i.e. assuming it to contain no statement superfluous to its solution) the relevant data and order could be inferred.

    What was the prescribed order of choosing; and who lent Caliban an umbrella?

    Solved by: Stephen Francis, DJ Williams & Hjallti

  10. Amazing Maze (Intermediate)

    This is a maze. The object is to start at the upper left hand corner, (1) and to make it to the lower right hand corner. A number says how far you must move to get to the next spot. You may move any direction, vertically, horizontally, or diagonally. A number that specifies movement off the board is a "dead end". 

    1   5   3   4   3   6   7   1   1   6

4   4   3   4   2   6   2   6   2   5

1   3   9   4   5   2   4   2   9   5

5   2   3   5   5   6   4   6   2   4

1   3   3   2   5   6   5   2   3   2

2   5   2   5   5   6   4   8   6   1

9   2   3   6   5   6   2   2   2   EXIT
    Solutions must be specified as a sequence of directions e.g. e, se, w, n, e ... The number of steps to be taken in a particular direction is determined by the value of that particular position in the maze. Thus for the given sequence we start by going one step east from the top left corner, then 5 steps south-east, then 5 steps west, and so on.

    Solved by: M.R.Vijayakumar, Stephen Miller & Maryse Sohier

  21. Dollar Number (Expert)

    Call a number n > 0 a "dollar number" if there is a way to make exactly one US dollar with exactly "n" US coins (denominations $.01, $.05, $.10, $.25, $.50, and $1.00). What is the smallest n > 0 that is not a dollar number?

    To make things clearer, for example, 4 is not a dollar number since 4 quarters make a dollar. 5 isn't a dollar number either because you can have .50 + 0.25 + 0.10 + 0.10 + 0.05 = 1.00.

    Solved by: Vishal Maru & Brian Cornish

  24. The Harvest Journey (Master)

    This puzzle is an original creation by John Tripp

    In a remote area, several villages are located, such that there is only one access road to each village,. This road connects each village with The Meeting Place where members of the villages meet to exchange goods, ideas, goodwill, etc.

    The highlight of the year is the Harvest Journey. After the harvest, all able bodied members of the villages travel to The Meeting Place for a great celebration. After the meeting, some will return to their own villages, while others will travel to one of the other villages to spend the winter.

    What is the maximum number of villages and the distance from The Meeting Place to each village?, if:

    1. A journey is the total number of halongs(The unit of distance used by the villagers.) from the Starting Village to The Meeting Place to the Ending Village. (Therefore, If there are 3 villages A,B,C, there would be six possible journeys - AA,AB,AC,BB,BC, and CC)
    2. Every possible journey is an integer number of halongs.
    3. No possible journey, from start to end, is the same distance as any other possible journey.
    4. No village is more than 100 halongs from The Meeting Place.
    5. A journey may begin and end at the same village.
    6. Each village is located as close to The Meeting Place as possible, consistent with conditions 1-5.

    Solved by: Brian Cornish (Congratulations!)
