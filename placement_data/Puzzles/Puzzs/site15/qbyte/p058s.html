Solution to puzzle 58: Fifth power plus five

We will use the Euclidean algorithm for polynomials.

Given polynomials f(x) and g(x) over a field K (such as the rationals or the reals), there exist unique polynomials q(x) and r(x) over K, such that:
f(x) = q(x) · g(x) + r(x),
where the degree of r is less than the degree of g, or r(x) = 0.
Further, the greatest common divisor of f and g divides r.

The same result holds if K is an integral domain (such as the integers), provided that g(x) is a monic polynomial; that is, the coefficient of the highest order term is 1.
In the working below, g will not always be monic, and so q and r will not be uniquely determined.  However, q and r will be unique, up to an integer multiple, and this is sufficient for us to determine the greatest common divisor of n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5.

Determining possible values for the greatest common divisor

In the following calculations, at each stage, we multiply both sides of the equation by the smallest integer required to ensure that each coefficient in q(x) is an integer.

(n + 1)5 + 5 = 1(n5 + 5) + (5n4 + 10n3 + 10n2 + 5n + 1).
5(n5 + 5) = (n - 2)(5n4 + 10n3 + 10n2 + 5n + 1) + (10n3 + 15n2 + 9n + 27).
4(5n4 + 10n3 + 10n2 + 5n + 1) = (2n + 1)(10n3 + 15n2 + 9n + 27) + (7n2 - 43n - 23).
98(10n3 + 15n2 + 9n + 27) = (140n + 1070)(7n2 - 43n - 23) + (50112n + 27256).
1569507840(7n2 - 43n - 23) = (219240n - 1466005)(50112n + 27256) + 3858751960.

Let d be the greatest common divisor of n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5.
Then d divides 3858751960.
The prime factorization of 3858751960 is 23 · 5 · 72 · 1968751.

Now, n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5 are alternately even and odd, and therefore cannot be congruent in any even modulus.
Similarly, by Fermat's Little Theorem, or by inspection, n5 = n (mod 5), and therefore n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5 cannot be congruent in any modulus that is a multiple of 5.
Finally, by inspection, n5 (n + 1)5 (mod 7), and so n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5 cannot be congruent in any modulus that is a multiple of 7.
Therefore d = 1 or d = 1968751.

When is the greatest common divisor > 1?

We also have d = greatest common divisor of 50112n + 27256 and 1968751.
Since 1968751 is prime, d = 1 if 50112n + 27256 0 (mod 1968751.)

To solve this congruence, we must find the modular inverse of 50112.  (We can speak of a unique modular inverse because the modulus, 1968751, is prime.)  In general, we would use the Euclidean algorithm to calculate a modular inverse.  However, in this case, the prime factors of 50112 are small, and we can proceed by inspection.

In fact, we have 50112 = 26 · 33 · 29.  The following calculations are understood to be performed modulo 1968751.

2 · 984376 = 1968751 + 1 2-1 984376.
Then 2-3 984376/4 246094.
So 2-6 2460942 1507325.

3 · 1312501 = 2 · 1968751 + 1 3-1 1312501.
Then 3-3 13125013 1239584.

Finally, 29 · 67888 = 1968751 + 1 29-1 67888.

Therefore 50112-1 1507325 · 1239584 · 67888 1731811.

We have 50112n + 27256 = 0, and so 50112n = 1941495.
Hence n = 1731811 · 1941495 533360 (mod 1968751.)

Conclusion

We conclude that n5 + 5 and (n + 1)5 + 5 are coprime if n 533360 (mod 1968751).
If n 533360 (mod 1968751), their greatest common divisor is 1968751.

(In fact, 5333605 + 5 = 5 · 78803 · 1968751 · 55641478729429717, and
5333615 + 5 = 2 · 31 · 1968751 · 549756587 · 643210846049.)
